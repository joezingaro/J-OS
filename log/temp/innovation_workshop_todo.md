### Slide 5: Framework 1 â€“ Rose, Thorn, Bud

**1. Framework Overview**
A retrospective tool used to audit current processes. It moves the team away from binary thinking ("Good vs. Bad") and into categorized thinking.

**2. Framework Delivery (The Sections)**
*   ðŸŒ¹ **Rose:** What is working well? (Strengths & Wins)
*   ðŸŒµ **Thorn:** What is painful or broken? (Friction & "Little i" opportunities)
*   ðŸŒ± **Bud:** What is a future opportunity? (Emerging ideas & "Big I" potential)

**3. Framework Features (Why it works)**
*   **Psychological Safety (The "Rose"):** We force positive feedback first to prevent a "gripe session." This lowers defenses so people feel safe sharing the negatives.
*   **Role Scalability (The "Thorn"):** Thorns scale with the user. A junior staff's Thorn is often "Little i" (broken task), while a VP's Thorn is often "Big I" (broken strategy).
*   **The Parking Lot (The "Bud"):** We separate "Buds" to keep dreamers from hijacking the meeting. It lets us focus on fixing the now (Thorns) without losing great ideas for the future.

> **Speaker Notes:**
>
> "When we run this exercise, pay attention to the Thorns. Thorns are the fuel for innovation."
>
> "If you are running this with your own team, do not skip the Roses. It validates their hard work and makes them receptive to discussing the Thorns."
>
> "Also, use the Buds column aggressively. If someone starts pitching a massive 5-year plan while you're trying to fix a spreadsheet, put it in 'Buds' and get back to work. Itâ€™s your parking lot."

### Slide 6: Framework 2 â€“ The Impact Matrix

**1. Framework Overview**
An adaptation of the Eisenhower Matrix used to prioritize our Thorns. It filters "Noise" from "Strategy."

**2. Framework Delivery (The Axes)**
*   **Y-Axis: Impact** (How much value does fixing this create?)
*   **X-Axis: Effort** (How hard is it to fix?)

**3. Framework Features (The Zones)**
*   **Top Left (High Impact / Low Effort):** Quick Wins. These are your "Little i" gold mines. Just do them.
*   **Top Right (High Impact / High Effort):** Major Projects. These are your strategic "Big I" PEP goals (e.g., Automation, New Systems).
*   **The Bottom Half:** Deprioritize. Do not waste innovation energy here.

> **Speaker Notes:**
>
> "A list of Thorns is overwhelming. We need to filter them."
>
> "When we move to the Matrix, we are looking for the top half of the board."
>
> "Top Left are things you should just fix tomorrow. Top Right are the things that become your annual goals."

### Slide 7: Framework 3 â€“ The 15% Solution

**1. Framework Overview**
A mindset shift that focuses on the 15% of control you have right now, rather than the 85% of constraints (budget, leadership, culture) you can't control.

**2. Framework Delivery (The Litmus Test)**
*   **The Question:** "What is the specific first step I can take tomorrow without asking for money or permission?"
*   **The Test:** If you cannot answer this question, you do not have a Goalâ€”you have a Wish or a Dependency.

**3. Framework Features (Why it works)**
*   **Locus of Control:** It shifts the mindset from "Victim" (waiting for IT to fix it) to "Owner" (gathering data to build the case).
*   **Momentum:** Innovation dies when we wait for the "perfect time." This forces us to take imperfect action immediately.
*   **Goal Validation:** It ensures your PEP goal is actionable.

> **Speaker Notes:**
>
> "This is the most important part of the day. We are using this as a Litmus Test for your goals."
>
> "If you say your goal is 'Fix the Software,' but you can't tell me what you are going to do about it tomorrow... that's not a goal. That's a wish."
>
> "But if you say, 'I will document the errors tomorrow to send to the vendor,' that is a 15% Solution. That is a valid goal."
